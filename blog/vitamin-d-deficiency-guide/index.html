Vitamin D Deficiency: Signs, Testing, and How to Fix It

A billion people worldwide are deficient in vitamin D — and most don't know it. Learn to recognize the signs, understand your risk factors, and know exactly when and how to supplement.

The Silent Deficiency

Vitamin D deficiency is called "the silent epidemic" for a reason: it develops slowly, the symptoms are nonspecific, and blood tests are rarely ordered until a problem is already significant.

The World Health Organization estimates that 1 billion people worldwide have insufficient vitamin D levels. In the United States, studies suggest 41% of adults are deficient (below 20 ng/mL) and over 70% fall below the 40 ng/mL level that many researchers consider optimal.

This matters because vitamin D isn't just a bone mineral — it's a steroid hormone that regulates hundreds of genes, modulates immune function, controls inflammatory pathways, and supports neurological and cardiovascular health.

Who Is Most at Risk?

Several populations face dramatically elevated deficiency risk:

Geographic location: UVB radiation — the wavelength that triggers vitamin D synthesis in skin — is only present at sufficient intensity between approximately 35°N and 35°S latitude, and only for a portion of the year even within those latitudes. If you live north of Los Angeles or Atlanta, you cannot synthesize adequate vitamin D from sunlight for 4–6 months of the year.

Indoor lifestyle: Office workers, students, and homebound individuals often go weeks without meaningful outdoor sun exposure. Even people in sunny climates can be deficient if they work indoors.

Darker skin tones: Melanin acts as a natural sunscreen, reducing UVB penetration. People with darker skin need 3–5x more sun exposure than those with lighter skin to synthesize the same amount of vitamin D. Studies consistently show significantly higher deficiency rates in Black and South Asian populations, even in sunny climates.

Age: Skin efficiency at synthesizing vitamin D declines by ~75% between age 20 and 70. Elderly adults are at extremely high risk regardless of sun exposure.

Obesity: Vitamin D is fat-soluble and gets sequestered in adipose (fat) tissue, reducing its bioavailability. People with obesity need substantially higher doses to achieve the same serum levels.

Malabsorption conditions: Celiac disease, Crohn's disease, and after bariatric surgery all significantly impair vitamin D absorption from food and supplements.

Dietary restriction: Vitamin D is found naturally in very few foods — primarily fatty fish, egg yolks, and organ meats. Vegans and people who avoid these foods get negligible dietary vitamin D.

Signs and Symptoms of Deficiency

Vitamin D deficiency rarely presents dramatically. The symptoms are often vague, overlapping, and easy to attribute to stress or aging. Common presentations include:

Physical Symptoms

  • Fatigue and low energy — one of the most consistent and underrecognized symptoms
  • Bone pain or achiness — particularly in the back, hips, and legs
  • Muscle weakness — difficulty climbing stairs, rising from chairs, or general physical de-conditioning
  • Frequent infections — respiratory infections in particular; multiple studies show D deficiency increases susceptibility
  • Slow wound healing — vitamin D plays a role in skin cell growth and immune-mediated repair
  • Hair loss — research suggests vitamin D receptors in hair follicles may mediate growth cycles

Neurological and Mood Symptoms

  • Depression and low mood — particularly seasonal affective disorder (SAD), which correlates strongly with winter vitamin D decline
  • Anxiety — less well-studied but biologically plausible via D's role in serotonin synthesis
  • Brain fog and poor concentration
  • Sleep disturbances — vitamin D receptors are found in brain regions that regulate sleep

The challenge: all of these symptoms are nonspecific. You can't reliably self-diagnose vitamin D deficiency from symptoms alone. Testing is the only way to know.

How to Test Your Vitamin D

The correct test is serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D (25(OH)D), sometimes written as "25-OH vitamin D" or "calcidiol."

Standard reference ranges vary by lab but typically classify:

  • Deficient: Below 20 ng/mL (50 nmol/L)
  • Insufficient: 20–30 ng/mL
  • Sufficient: 30–50 ng/mL
  • Optimal (by many functional medicine standards): 40–60 ng/mL
  • Toxic: Above 100 ng/mL (very rare with supplementation at normal doses)

You can request this test from your physician, or order it directly through services like Everly Health or Quest Direct for $50–100. Testing once a year (ideally in late winter, when levels are at their annual low) is a reasonable approach for most adults.

How to Supplement Effectively

For most deficient adults, 2,000–4,000 IU of vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol) per day is a reasonable maintenance dose. This is well below the upper tolerable intake of 10,000 IU/day established by most health authorities.

Key Points on Supplementation

D3 vs D2: Always choose vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol). D3 is 2–3x more effective at raising serum 25(OH)D levels compared to D2 (ergocalciferol). Vegan D3 from lichen is now widely available and performs identically to animal-derived D3.

Take with fat: Vitamin D is fat-soluble — absorption is significantly improved when taken with a fat-containing meal. A 2015 study found absorption improved 32% when taken with the largest meal of the day.

Pair with K2: Vitamin D3 increases calcium absorption. Vitamin K2 (specifically MK-7) activates the proteins that direct that calcium to bones rather than arteries. Taking D3 without K2 may not be optimal for cardiovascular health long-term.

Pair with magnesium: Magnesium is required to convert vitamin D to its active form. Deficiency in magnesium can limit the effectiveness of vitamin D supplementation.

Dosing for Deficiency Correction

If you're confirmed deficient (below 20 ng/mL), higher loading doses are typically used under medical supervision:

  • 5,000–10,000 IU/day for 8–12 weeks, then retest
  • Or a single high-dose "loading" protocol (100,000–300,000 IU) in some clinical settings

These higher doses should be used with physician oversight and followed by retesting.

The Bottom Line

Vitamin D deficiency is extraordinarily common, frequently undiagnosed, and completely correctable. If you have any of the risk factors above — especially indoor lifestyle, darker skin tone, northern latitude, or age over 50 — getting your levels tested is one of the most valuable health assessments you can make.

If testing isn't immediately accessible, supplementing at 2,000–4,000 IU/day is considered safe for most adults and is unlikely to cause harm while being highly likely to help.
