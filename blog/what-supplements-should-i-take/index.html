What Supplements Should I Take? A Personalized, Evidence-Based Guide

With thousands of supplements on the market, deciding what to take can be overwhelming. Here's a systematic, evidence-based approach to figuring out exactly what your body needs.

The Supplement Paradox

Walk into any health food store or open Amazon, and you'll find thousands of supplements promising to fix everything from fatigue to aging. The supplement industry is a $177 billion market — and most of it is built on confusion.

Here's the honest truth: most people are taking supplements they don't need, and missing the ones they actually do. A blanket multivitamin doesn't solve a magnesium deficiency caused by chronic stress. A high-dose vitamin C isn't going to help the fatigue caused by undiagnosed B12 deficiency in a vegan.

Personalization is everything. The supplements that are right for you depend on four intersecting factors: your health goals, your diet, your lifestyle, and your symptoms.

Factor 1: Your Health Goals

Your goals give a starting direction — but they're the weakest signal of the four. Everyone wants more energy and better sleep; that doesn't automatically mean everyone needs the same supplements.

That said, goals do matter when used to filter your shortlist:

  • Energy: Look at B vitamins (especially B12), iron, CoQ10, and Vitamin D
  • Sleep: Magnesium glycinate, melatonin, and ashwagandha all have RCT evidence for sleep
  • Muscle and recovery: Creatine monohydrate has the most evidence of any sports supplement
  • Immunity: Vitamin D3, zinc, and Vitamin C have the strongest immune support data
  • Focus and cognition: Omega-3 (DHA specifically), creatine, and B12 top the evidence list
  • Longevity: Omega-3, NAC, CoQ10, and magnesium have the strongest aging-related data

Factor 2: Your Diet — The Strongest Signal

Your diet is the single most powerful predictor of which supplements you need. Dietary patterns create predictable, systematic deficiencies that are well-documented in the scientific literature.

Vegan and vegetarian diets have the most clearly defined gaps:

  • Vitamin B12 is essentially zero in plant foods. Studies show 93–95% of vegans are deficient without supplementation. This is non-negotiable.
  • Omega-3 (EPA/DHA): ALA from flaxseed converts to EPA at only 5–10% efficiency. Algae oil provides direct EPA and DHA — the same source fish get theirs from.
  • Iron: Non-heme iron from plants is far less bioavailable than heme iron from meat. Deficiency is common, especially in women.
  • Zinc: Plant phytates inhibit zinc absorption. Vegetarians may need 50% more zinc than omnivores.
  • Creatine: Found exclusively in animal muscle tissue. Vegetarians start with significantly lower muscle and brain creatine stores.

Keto and carnivore diets create different gaps:

  • Vitamin C (animal foods contain very little)
  • Magnesium and potassium (restricted fruit and vegetable intake)
  • Folate/methylfolate (from leafy greens, largely absent)

Standard Western diets typically fall short on:

  • Magnesium (soil depletion + processed food dominance)
  • Omega-3 (ratio of omega-6 to omega-3 is 15:1 in the average American diet vs. the optimal 4:1)
  • Vitamin D

Factor 3: Your Lifestyle

Several lifestyle factors reliably drive up demand for specific nutrients.

Sun exposure: If you work indoors, live above 35° latitude, or use high-SPF sunscreen daily, your Vitamin D synthesis is severely limited. Studies show indoor office workers have average serum 25(OH)D levels well below the 40–60 ng/mL optimal range.

Exercise: Intense exercise, especially cardio, increases iron loss through sweat, hemolysis, and GI bleeding. Weight training dramatically increases demand for creatine, magnesium, and protein synthesis cofactors.

Stress: Chronic stress depletes magnesium rapidly via adrenal activation. The more stressed you are, the more magnesium you burn through — creating a vicious cycle. Ashwagandha (KSM-66) has RCT evidence for cortisol reduction.

Alcohol: Even moderate alcohol consumption significantly increases urinary excretion of magnesium, zinc, and B vitamins — particularly folate and B12.

Caffeine: Regular caffeine intake increases urinary excretion of calcium, magnesium, and B vitamins. Heavy coffee drinkers should prioritize magnesium.

Factor 4: Your Symptoms — Reading the Signals

Symptoms are valuable clues, but they're not definitive diagnostic tools. Many nutrient deficiencies share overlapping symptoms. Still, certain patterns are worth noting:

  • Persistent fatigue despite adequate sleep: Consider B12, iron, Vitamin D, CoQ10
  • Poor sleep quality or difficulty falling asleep: Magnesium deficiency is among the most common causes
  • Brain fog and poor concentration: B12, omega-3 DHA, and Vitamin D are common culprits
  • Frequent colds and infections: Vitamin D, zinc, and Vitamin C top the list
  • Muscle cramps and weakness: Classic magnesium deficiency symptoms
  • Hair loss: Often associated with iron, zinc, or biotin deficiency
  • Low mood: Vitamin D, omega-3 EPA, and B12 all have evidence for mood support

The Tier-1 Shortlist: Where to Start

If you're overwhelmed and want a starting point, these are the supplements that consistently show up across population-level data as most commonly deficient and most impactful:

1. Vitamin D3 + K2 — Deficient in ~70% of adults in the developed world. Take with a fat-containing meal.

2. Magnesium Glycinate — Deficient in ~68% of Americans. Essential for sleep, stress, and muscle function. View on Amazon

3. Omega-3 (fish oil or algae oil) — Unless you eat fatty fish 3x/week, you're likely deficient in EPA and DHA. View on Amazon

4. Vitamin B12 — Essential for vegans/vegetarians; worth checking for anyone over 50.

Beyond these, your specific needs depend on the four factors above.

How to Build Your Personal Protocol

Rather than guessing, the most effective approach is systematic:

1. Start with diet assessment — What are the documented gaps in your dietary pattern?

2. Layer in lifestyle factors — Low sun, high stress, intense exercise, alcohol use?

3. Map symptoms to likely deficiencies — What patterns match?

4. Cross-reference goals — Which supplements support your primary goals AND address likely gaps?

5. Prioritize overlap — The supplements that appear in multiple categories (e.g., magnesium for sleep AND stress AND exercise) should be your first priorities.

What to Avoid

Not all supplements are created equal. A few red flags:

  • Proprietary blends: Undisclosed doses make it impossible to know if you're getting a therapeutic amount
  • "Mega-dose" formulas: More is not always better. Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) can accumulate to toxic levels
  • Under-dosed "label dressing": Many multivitamins include nutrients at 1–10% of effective doses just to list them on the label
  • Unproven botanicals: Dozens of popular supplements (echinacea, green tea extract, resveratrol) have weak or inconsistent clinical evidence despite heavy marketing

Bottom Line

The right supplements for you are not the same as the right supplements for your partner, your friend, or the influencer you follow. Your diet, lifestyle, and physiology create a unique nutrient fingerprint.

The most evidence-backed approach: identify your likely deficiencies based on diet and lifestyle, cross-reference with your symptoms and goals, and prioritize the supplements that show up multiple times.
